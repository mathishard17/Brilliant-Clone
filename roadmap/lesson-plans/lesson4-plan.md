# Lesson 4 Plan: Magic Chance Spinners

## Title and concept

**Title:** Lesson 4: Magic Chance Spinners

**Concept:** Introductory probability as:

```text
chance = favorable outcomes / total outcomes
```

The learner should leave with one big idea: **chance is counting**. To find the chance of a royal prize, count the spaces that win that prize, then count all the spaces that could happen.

Target length: **5-10 minutes**.

Target learner: **3rd grade**, concrete visual reasoning, princess/royal/fantasy theme.

Do not introduce decimals, percentages, simplifying fractions, randomness simulations, or AI-generated/personalized curriculum features in this lesson.

## Why it follows Lessons 1-3

Lessons 1-3 teach the learner how to count possible outcomes:

- **Lesson 1:** Every crown can pair with every gown, so we can count all outfit outcomes.
- **Lesson 2:** Royal lineups change when order matters, so we can count arranged outcomes.
- **Lesson 3:** Treasure groups do not change when order does not matter, so we can count chosen outcomes without double-counting.

Lesson 4 uses those counting skills for chance. Instead of asking, "How many ways are possible?" it asks, "Out of all the possible outcomes, how many are the ones we want?"

This is the natural bridge from combinatorics to probability:

```text
favorable outcomes / total outcomes
```

## Learning goals

By the end of the mini lesson, the learner can:

- Identify an **outcome** as one thing that can happen after a spin, draw, or pick.
- Count the **total outcomes** on a spinner, in a bag, or on a prize wheel.
- Count **favorable outcomes**, meaning the outcomes the princess is hoping for.
- Write simple probabilities as fractions like **1/4**, **2/6**, and **3/8**.
- Recognize **impossible** as **0/total** and **certain** as **total/total**.
- Compare simple chances using words like **more likely**, **less likely**, and **same chance**.

## Learner story/theme

The princess is preparing the **Royal Moonlight Carnival** at the castle. A friendly fairy has brought a magic prize spinner that can award crowns, rubies, gowns, dragon stickers, and sparkle stars.

The fairy says:

> "A magic spinner may feel surprising, but its chance is not a secret spell. Count the winning spaces, count all the spaces, and you can tell how likely the prize is."

The learner helps the princess inspect the carnival games before guests arrive. The goal is not to make the spinner "fair" yet. That belongs in Lesson 5. The goal here is simply to count chance.

## Section-by-section flow

### 1. One Spin, One Prize

**Purpose:** Introduce outcomes.

**Screen type:** Interactive visualization plus numeric challenge.

**Visual:** A 4-space castle spinner with equal spaces:

- Crown
- Ruby
- Gown
- Dragon

**Learner action:** Tap **Spin** once or tap spaces to inspect them. The app can animate a spin, but the question should be answerable by looking at the spinner.

**Teaching copy:**

> Welcome to the Royal Moonlight Carnival! This magic spinner has 4 spaces. When the princess spins, the arrow lands on exactly one space. Each space is one possible **outcome**.

**Challenge question:**

> How many total outcomes are on this spinner?

**Answer:** `4`

**Feedback:**

- Correct: `Sparkly counting! There are 4 spaces, so there are 4 total outcomes.`
- Try again: `Try again! Count every space the arrow could land on.`
- Incorrect/solution: `There are 4 possible outcomes: Crown, Ruby, Gown, and Dragon.`

**Key line after challenge:**

> The **total outcomes** are all the things that could happen.

### 2. Winning Spaces

**Purpose:** Introduce favorable outcomes and the probability fraction.

**Screen type:** Clickthrough explanation followed by numeric challenge.

**Visual:** A 6-space spinner:

- Ruby
- Ruby
- Crown
- Gown
- Dragon
- Star

**Teaching copy:**

> The princess hopes to win a Ruby. The spinner has 6 total spaces, but only 2 Ruby spaces.
>
> The Ruby spaces are the **favorable outcomes**. They are the outcomes we are hoping for.

**Equation reveal:**

```text
chance of Ruby = Ruby spaces / total spaces
chance of Ruby = 2 / 6
```

**Challenge question:**

> What is the chance of spinning a Ruby?

**Answer:** `2/6`

Acceptable implementation answer options:

- Prefer multiple choice for this first fraction question:
  - A. `1/6`
  - B. `2/6`
  - C. `4/6`
  - D. `6/2`

**Correct answer:** `B`

**Feedback:**

- Correct: `Yes! There are 2 Ruby spaces out of 6 total spaces, so the chance is 2/6.`
- Try again: `Try again! Put the winning Ruby spaces on top and all spinner spaces on bottom.`
- Incorrect/solution: `The princess wants Ruby. There are 2 Ruby spaces and 6 total spaces, so the chance is 2/6.`

**Key line after challenge:**

> Probability is a counting fraction: **favorable outcomes over total outcomes**.

### 3. More Likely, Less Likely

**Purpose:** Compare probabilities without decimals.

**Screen type:** Interactive inspect-and-compare challenge.

**Visual:** A 6-space spinner:

- Star
- Star
- Star
- Crown
- Crown
- Dragon

**Teaching copy:**

> Some prizes have more winning spaces than others. More winning spaces means a prize is **more likely**.

**Challenge question:**

> Which prize is more likely: Star or Dragon?

**Answer:** `Star`

**Feedback:**

- Correct: `Exactly! Star has 3 winning spaces out of 6. Dragon has only 1 winning space out of 6. Star is more likely.`
- Try again: `Try again! Count how many spaces show each prize.`
- Incorrect/solution: `Star is more likely because Star has 3 spaces and Dragon has 1 space. That means Star has a chance of 3/6, while Dragon has a chance of 1/6.`

**Follow-up mini challenge:**

> What is the chance of spinning a Crown?

**Answer:** `2/6`

**Feedback:**

- Correct: `Royal work! There are 2 Crown spaces out of 6 total spaces, so the chance is 2/6.`
- Try again: `Count only the Crown spaces first, then count all the spaces.`
- Incorrect/solution: `There are 2 Crown spaces and 6 total spaces, so the chance is 2/6.`

### 4. Impossible and Certain Magic

**Purpose:** Teach 0/total and total/total with concrete examples.

**Screen type:** Clickthrough explanation with two quick checks.

**Visual A:** A 5-space bag or spinner with:

- Crown
- Crown
- Ruby
- Ruby
- Star

No Dragon appears.

**Teaching copy A:**

> The princess asks for a Dragon prize, but look carefully: there are no Dragon spaces. If there are 0 winning spaces, the chance is **0/5**. That means impossible on this spinner.

**Challenge question A:**

> What is the chance of getting a Dragon from this spinner?

**Answer:** `0/5`

**Feedback A:**

- Correct: `Correct! There are 0 Dragon spaces out of 5 total spaces, so Dragon is impossible here.`
- Try again: `Try again! How many Dragon spaces do you see?`
- Incorrect/solution: `There are no Dragon spaces. That means 0 favorable outcomes out of 5 total outcomes, or 0/5.`

**Visual B:** A 4-space spinner where every space is Sparkle:

- Sparkle
- Sparkle
- Sparkle
- Sparkle

**Teaching copy B:**

> Now every space is Sparkle. If every possible outcome wins, the chance is **4/4**. That means certain.

**Challenge question B:**

> What is the chance of spinning Sparkle?

**Answer:** `4/4`

**Feedback B:**

- Correct: `Perfect! All 4 spaces are Sparkle, so Sparkle is certain: 4/4.`
- Try again: `Try again! Count the Sparkle spaces, then count all spaces.`
- Incorrect/solution: `There are 4 Sparkle spaces out of 4 total spaces, so the chance is 4/4.`

### 5. Royal Prize Wheel Finale

**Purpose:** Combine counting favorable outcomes, total outcomes, and comparison.

**Screen type:** Final interactive challenge.

**Visual:** An 8-space castle prize wheel:

- Crown
- Crown
- Crown
- Ruby
- Ruby
- Dragon
- Gown
- Star

**Teaching copy:**

> The final carnival wheel is ready! The princess wants to know the chance before the first guest spins. Count the prize spaces like a royal mathematician.

**Challenge question 1:**

> What is the chance of spinning a Crown?

**Answer:** `3/8`

**Feedback:**

- Correct: `Beautiful! There are 3 Crown spaces out of 8 total spaces, so the chance is 3/8.`
- Try again: `Try again! Count the Crown spaces first, then count every space on the wheel.`
- Incorrect/solution: `There are 3 favorable Crown spaces and 8 total spaces. The chance is 3/8.`

**Challenge question 2:**

> Which is more likely: Ruby or Dragon?

**Answer:** `Ruby`

**Feedback:**

- Correct: `Yes! Ruby has 2 spaces out of 8, while Dragon has 1 space out of 8. Ruby is more likely.`
- Try again: `Try again! Count Ruby spaces and Dragon spaces separately.`
- Incorrect/solution: `Ruby has 2 favorable spaces. Dragon has 1 favorable space. Since 2/8 is more than 1/8, Ruby is more likely.`

**Final lesson message:**

> You did it! Chance is not just a mystery spell. It is a counting fraction: **favorable outcomes over total outcomes**.

## Interactive visualization ideas

Use one primary visualization if build time is tight: a reusable **ChanceSpinner** component.

Recommended interactions:

- **Spinner inspection:** Learner taps each wedge; selected wedges glow and the app shows a running count like `Ruby spaces: 2`.
- **Spin button:** Optional animation that lands on a wedge. Keep the lesson questions based on the visible spaces, not on random experimental results.
- **Winning-space highlight:** When the learner chooses a target prize, all matching spaces pulse.
- **Fraction builder:** Two slots labeled `winning spaces` and `total spaces`; the learner drags or taps counts into `2 / 6`.
- **Castle prize wheel:** A larger finale spinner with 8 equal wedges and royal icons.
- **Magic treasure bag variant:** A bag with visible tokens can teach the same idea. Pulling a token may be animated, but the probability question should still use visible counts.

Keep all outcomes **equally likely** in Lesson 4. Use equal-size spinner wedges and identical bag tokens. Unequal spinner sections can be a later extension, not the core lesson.

## Implementation notes for current React lesson registry architecture

Keep implementation aligned with the current lesson pattern:

- Add a future `src/lessons/lesson4/copy.ts` for static copy, challenge definitions, answers, and feedback strings.
- Add a future `src/lessons/lesson4/screens.tsx` with about 5 screens matching the flow above.
- Add a new lesson id in `src/types/lesson.ts`, likely `LESSON_4_ID = 'lesson-4-magic-chance-spinners'`.
- Extend `LESSON_DEFINITIONS` in `src/lessons/registry.ts` with title, emoji, description, progress steps, lazy screen imports, and screen numbers.
- Suggested registry metadata:
  - `title`: `Lesson 4: Magic Chance Spinners`
  - `emoji`: a sparkle/star or crystal ball style emoji if the app continues using emoji labels
  - `description`: `Count winning spaces to discover chance`
  - `progressSteps`: `['Outcomes', 'Winning Spaces', 'More Likely', 'Impossible/Certain', 'Final Wheel']`
- The current `InteractiveVisualizationKind` union does not include probability visuals. Add future values such as:
  - `chance-spinner`
  - `chance-spinner-compare`
  - `chance-spinner-impossible-certain`
  - `castle-prize-wheel`
- The current `LessonProgress` shape is shared and still named around screens 1-3 from Lesson 1. A future Lesson 4 implementation may either:
  - keep progress lightweight and reuse current screen-level progress only if the app treats clickthrough progress generically, or
  - introduce lesson-specific progress fields for selected target prize, selected answer, spin count, and completed challenge states.
- Prefer multiple choice or button answers for early fraction questions. Free-text fraction input like `2/6` can create avoidable parsing issues for a 3rd-grade lesson.
- Do not depend on random spin results for correctness. Random spins are fun animation, but the math should come from inspecting the visible sample space.
- Keep all data local and static. This lesson does not need Firebase, personalization, generated curriculum, or backend calls.

## Stretch ideas, clearly optional

These are optional and should not block the core Lesson 4 build:

- Add a **"make your own spinner"** sandbox after completion where the learner chooses how many Crown, Ruby, and Dragon spaces appear, then the app shows the probability fractions.
- Add a **bag of jewels** alternate visualization using visible tokens: `3 Ruby`, `2 Sapphire`, `1 Emerald`.
- Add a gentle **fraction simplification sparkle** only as a visual note, such as `2/6 is the same size chance as 1/3`, but do not require simplification.
- Add a **spin history ribbon** showing the last 5 spins to hint that experiments can be surprising even when counts tell the expected chance.
- Save fair/unfair spinner design for Lesson 5, where comparing players' chances becomes the main idea.
