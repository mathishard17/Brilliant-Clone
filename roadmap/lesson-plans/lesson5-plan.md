# Lesson 5: Fair Games at the Royal Carnival

## Title and Concept

**Title:** Fair Games at the Royal Carnival

**Concept:** Sample spaces and fairness.

**Core intuition:** A game is fair when each player has the same chance to win. To check fairness, first list all possible outcomes, then count how many outcomes help each player.

**Target length:** 5-10 minutes.

**Grade level:** 3rd grade, using small counts, visual outcomes, and royal carnival story language.

## Why It Follows Lessons 1-4

Lesson 5 uses every earlier idea in a simple, playful way:

- From Lesson 1, learners know that choices can combine to make total outcomes.
- From Lesson 2, learners know that different orders or arrangements can create different results.
- From Lesson 3, learners know that sometimes two things that look different at first are actually the same choice.
- From Lesson 4, learners know that probability compares favorable outcomes to total outcomes.

This lesson turns those skills into a new question: "Is this game fair?"

The key step is naming the **sample space**: the full list of possible outcomes. Once the sample space is visible, fairness becomes a counting question.

## Learning Goals

By the end of the lesson, the learner should be able to:

- Explain that a **sample space** is the full list of possible outcomes.
- Count total outcomes in a small spinner or two-step game.
- Count favorable outcomes for each player.
- Decide whether a game is fair or unfair by comparing chances.
- Fix a simple unfair game by changing the outcome spaces.

## Learner Story/Theme

The princess is opening a Royal Carnival in the castle courtyard. There are ribbon booths, jewel prizes, dragon snacks, and a magic spinner game.

The princess wants everyone to have fun, but the royal dragon notices something suspicious: some games might give one player a better chance than the other.

The learner becomes the **Royal Fairness Checker**. Their job is to inspect each carnival game, list the possible outcomes, and help the princess make games fair before the gates open.

Keep the story concrete:

- Princess player wins on crowns or stars.
- Dragon or knight player wins on dragons or shields.
- Jewel spaces can be "no one wins" spaces.
- Spinner spaces must be equal size when comparing probabilities.

## Section-by-Section Flow

### 1. Carnival Hook: Is This Game Fair?

**Time:** 1 minute.

The learner sees a carnival spinner with 4 equal spaces:

- Crown
- Crown
- Dragon
- Jewel

Story copy:

> Welcome to the Royal Carnival, {princessName}! The princess made a spinner game, but the dragon says, "Wait! Does everyone have the same chance to win?"

Interaction:

- Show the spinner.
- Let the learner tap each space.
- Tapping a space adds it to a visible "possible outcomes" tray.

Math idea:

- Each equal spinner space is one possible outcome.
- If the same picture appears twice, it counts twice because the spinner has two spaces where it can land.

### 2. Build the Sample Space

**Time:** 1-2 minutes.

Introduce the vocabulary:

> The **sample space** is the list of everything that could happen.

The sample space for the spinner is:

`Crown, Crown, Dragon, Jewel`

Interaction:

- Learner taps "List outcomes."
- The app reveals 4 cards under the spinner.
- Duplicate Crown cards are shown separately, maybe labeled Crown 1 and Crown 2.

Math idea:

- Total outcomes: `4`.
- Favorable outcomes for the princess if she wins on Crown: `2`.
- Her chance is `2 out of 4`.

### 3. Fair or Unfair?

**Time:** 1-2 minutes.

Rules:

- Princess wins if the spinner lands on Crown.
- Dragon wins if the spinner lands on Dragon.
- Jewel means no one wins.

The learner compares:

- Princess: `2` winning spaces
- Dragon: `1` winning space

Conclusion:

> This game is unfair because the princess has more winning spaces than the dragon.

Interaction:

- Show a "Fairness Meter" with Princess 2/4 and Dragon 1/4.
- If counts match, meter says "Fair."
- If counts do not match, meter says "Unfair."

### 4. Fix the Carnival Game

**Time:** 2-3 minutes.

The learner gets a simple game builder with 4 equal spinner spaces:

Starting spinner:

`Crown, Crown, Crown, Dragon`

Rules:

- Princess wins on Crown.
- Dragon wins on Dragon.

Goal:

> Repaint the fewest spaces so both players have the same chance to win.

Expected learner action:

- Change 1 Crown space into 1 Dragon space.
- New spinner: `Crown, Crown, Dragon, Dragon`.

Math idea:

- Princess has `2/4`.
- Dragon has `2/4`.
- Same chance means fair.

### 5. Royal Review: Two-Step Game

**Time:** 1-2 minutes.

Use a tiny two-step sample space to connect back to Lesson 1.

Game:

- Spinner A can land on Crown or Dragon.
- Spinner B can land on Red or Blue.

Sample space:

- Crown + Red
- Crown + Blue
- Dragon + Red
- Dragon + Blue

Math idea:

- `2 choices x 2 choices = 4 outcomes`.
- A sample space can come from more than one spinner.

Final fairness check:

- Princess wins if the two royal pictures match: Crown + Crown or Dragon + Dragon.
- Knight wins if they do not match: Crown + Dragon or Dragon + Crown.
- There are 2 matching outcomes and 2 non-matching outcomes, so the game is fair.

For a 5-minute version, use Sections 1, 2, 3, and 4. For a 10-minute version, include Section 5 and all challenge checks.

## Interactive Visualization Ideas

### Spinner Inspector

Purpose: Help the learner see that the sample space is made of equal physical spaces, not just different labels.

Behavior:

- Render a 4-space spinner with clear equal sections.
- On tap, highlight a section and add its label to the sample-space tray.
- If two spaces both say Crown, show both cards separately.
- Add a small total counter: `Total outcomes: 4`.

### Sample-Space Tray

Purpose: Make "all possible outcomes" concrete.

Behavior:

- Cards appear in a row: `Crown`, `Crown`, `Dragon`, `Jewel`.
- Duplicate outcomes are not merged.
- Favorable cards can glow when the learner selects a player.

### Fairness Meter

Purpose: Turn fairness into a visual comparison.

Behavior:

- Left side: Princess winning outcomes.
- Right side: Dragon or Knight winning outcomes.
- If counts match, show "Fair: same chance."
- If counts differ, show "Unfair: one player has more winning spaces."

### Fair Spinner Builder

Purpose: Let the learner fix a game, not just answer about one.

Behavior:

- Four editable equal spinner spaces.
- Learner taps a space to cycle through Crown, Dragon, Jewel.
- App checks whether Princess and Dragon have equal winning counts.
- Keep the target small: 4 or 6 spaces only.

### Two-Spinner Sample-Space Grid

Purpose: Bridge from Lesson 1 multiplication to sample spaces.

Behavior:

- Show Spinner A choices as rows.
- Show Spinner B choices as columns.
- Fill each grid cell with a possible outcome.
- For 3rd grade, keep it to `2 x 2`.

## Exact Challenge Questions, Answers, and Feedback

### Challenge 1: Count the Sample Space

**Prompt:** The Royal Carnival spinner has 4 equal spaces: Crown, Crown, Dragon, and Jewel. How many total possible landing spaces are in the sample space?

**Answer:** `4`

**Correct feedback:** Royal work, {princessName}! The sample space has 4 outcomes because the spinner has 4 equal spaces.

**Incorrect feedback:** Not quite. Count the spinner spaces one by one, even when two spaces have the same picture.

**Try-again feedback:** Try again! How many equal spaces can the spinner land on?

**Solution feedback:** Solution: `Crown, Crown, Dragon, Jewel` makes 4 possible outcomes.

### Challenge 2: Count Favorable Outcomes

**Prompt:** The princess wins when the spinner lands on Crown. The sample space is Crown, Crown, Dragon, Jewel. How many favorable outcomes does the princess have?

**Answer:** `2`

**Correct feedback:** Exactly, {princessName}! Crown appears on 2 spinner spaces, so the princess has 2 favorable outcomes.

**Incorrect feedback:** Not quite. Look for every space that says Crown. Both Crown spaces count.

**Try-again feedback:** Try again! Count the Crown spaces only.

**Solution feedback:** Solution: there are 2 Crown spaces, so the princess has `2 out of 4` chances to win.

### Challenge 3: Decide Fair or Unfair

**Prompt:** Princess wins on Crown. Dragon wins on Dragon. The spinner is Crown, Crown, Dragon, Jewel. Is the game fair? Type `1` for fair or `2` for unfair.

**Answer:** `2`

**Correct feedback:** Correct! This game is unfair because Princess has 2 winning spaces and Dragon has only 1.

**Incorrect feedback:** Not quite. A fair game gives each player the same number of winning outcomes.

**Try-again feedback:** Try again! Compare Princess winning spaces to Dragon winning spaces.

**Solution feedback:** Solution: Princess has `2/4`, Dragon has `1/4`, and Jewel is no winner. The chances are not the same, so the game is unfair.

### Challenge 4: Fix the Spinner

**Prompt:** A spinner has Crown, Crown, Crown, Dragon. Princess wins on Crown, and Dragon wins on Dragon. How many Crown spaces should change to Dragon spaces to make the game fair?

**Answer:** `1`

**Correct feedback:** Perfect fix, {princessName}! Changing 1 Crown into Dragon gives Crown, Crown, Dragon, Dragon.

**Incorrect feedback:** Not quite. The goal is for Princess and Dragon to have the same number of winning spaces.

**Try-again feedback:** Try again! Can you make 2 Crown spaces and 2 Dragon spaces?

**Solution feedback:** Solution: repaint 1 Crown as Dragon. Then Princess has `2/4` and Dragon has `2/4`, so the game is fair.

### Challenge 5: Check a Built Game

**Prompt:** Your carnival builder has 6 equal spaces. You made 3 Star spaces for Princess and 3 Shield spaces for Knight. Is the game fair? Type `1` for fair or `2` for unfair.

**Answer:** `1`

**Correct feedback:** Yes! Princess has 3 winning spaces and Knight has 3 winning spaces, so they have the same chance.

**Incorrect feedback:** Not quite. Count each player's winning spaces and compare them.

**Try-again feedback:** Try again! Does 3 match 3?

**Solution feedback:** Solution: Princess has `3/6`, Knight has `3/6`, and those chances are equal. The game is fair.

### Challenge 6: Final Two-Spinner Fairness Check

**Prompt:** Two tiny royal spinners each have Crown and Dragon. Princess wins if the two pictures match: Crown + Crown or Dragon + Dragon. Knight wins if they do not match: Crown + Dragon or Dragon + Crown. Is the game fair? Type `1` for fair or `2` for unfair.

**Answer:** `1`

**Correct feedback:** Brilliant checking, {princessName}! There are 2 matching outcomes and 2 not-matching outcomes, so the game is fair.

**Incorrect feedback:** Not quite. Write out the full sample space before deciding.

**Try-again feedback:** Try again! List all 4 outcomes, then count matches and not-matches.

**Solution feedback:** Solution: the sample space is `Crown+Crown`, `Crown+Dragon`, `Dragon+Crown`, `Dragon+Dragon`. Princess wins 2 outcomes and Knight wins 2 outcomes, so the game is fair.

## Stretch Ideas, Clearly Optional

These are optional and should not be part of the first implementation unless the core lesson is already working well.

- Add a decorative "Spin 10 times" experiment after the fairness check, then compare the messy experiment results to the exact sample-space counts.
- Add a dice-sum carnival game where Princess wins on even sums and Dragon wins on odd sums. This is more advanced because the sample space is larger.
- Add a "design your own prize booth" mode with 6 equal spaces and two player symbols.
- Add a fairness puzzle with a no-winner space, such as Crown, Dragon, Jewel, Jewel, to show that a game can be fair between two players even if neither player wins every time.
- Add gentle expected-intuition language: "If we played many times, who would probably win more often?" Avoid formal expected value.
